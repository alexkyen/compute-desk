How to read the field

The log axis is the point of this view. On a linear axis, the difference between $1.50 and $3 disappears next to the difference between $10 and $12 — but to a buyer they are the same event: a 2× spread. Log scale makes ratios distances, so the three archetype bands sit at roughly constant heights across generations: marketplaces at the floor, neoclouds in a tight middle band, hyperscaler list floating 2–6× above it for identical silicon. The useful question is rarely what a price is — it's which band it belongs to. see it on the chart · 01 ↑log scale: equal distances are equal ratios — a 2× gap reads the same at $2 as at $10.

The wall of ×. Points thin out and × marks thicken as you move right — that gradient is the market's defining fact in one glance. Hopper is a market; Blackwell is an allocation; the rack-scale rows and alt silicon are almost pure ×. The emptying field is itself the datum: the newer the silicon, the more of the market moves behind closed doors.

Tracing a seller draws their curve per channel across generations — solid through on-demand, dashed through committed, dotted through spot. The vertical gap between a seller's solid and dashed lines is their negotiation spread made visible: OCI's list-to-deal gap is a canyon, Nebius's is a step. A seller whose trace simply stops at H200 has nothing public beyond Hopper — which is itself a fact about how they sell. see it on the chart · 02 ↑grades: list · mkt · deal · est — every print carries one; defined under sources & method below.

What the field compresses. Published ranges collapse to a midpoint with a whisker, and the nuance behind a gated offering ("on card", "roadmap", "pre-alloc") survives only in the × tooltips. The field is built for shape and negotiation; when you need the exact number to put in a model, follow the tooltip to its source.
