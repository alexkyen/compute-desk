Reading the wiring like a buyer

The hierarchy is the whole story. Inside one node, GPUs talk over NVLink at hundreds of GB/s. The moment a job outgrows the node, traffic drops onto the network at NIC speed — 400 Gb/s (50 GB/s) per GPU in a standard H100 build — an order of magnitude slower. Training synchronizes gradients across every GPU each step (the all-reduce), so for any model too large for one node, the network is in the inner loop and its bandwidth prices the whole cluster.

InfiniBand vs. RoCE is a protocol choice, not a speed choice. Both run the same wire rates; both carry RDMA. InfiniBand is lossless by construction (credit-based flow control, sub-microsecond switching) and historically delivers ~90%+ of line rate to NCCL collectives. RoCEv2 is RDMA over Ethernet made lossless with PFC and ECN — cheaper, multi-vendor, easier to operate at hyperscale, but its delivered efficiency depends heavily on tuning and congestion control. Well-engineered Ethernet fabrics (NVIDIA's own Spectrum-X, and the Ultra Ethernet effort) claim to close to within a few points of InfiniBand; untuned RoCE can fall far below. The buyer's conclusion: never accept line rate as an answer — demand measured NCCL bus bandwidth at your job size.

Oversubscription is a quiet discount you pay, not receive. A 2:1 fabric has half as much uplink capacity out of each leaf as downlink under it — fine for inference microservices that rarely cross leaves, poisonous for training all-reduces that cross constantly. It's invisible on the price sheet and only appears in the topology diagram, which is precisely why you ask for the topology diagram. Corollary: placement is free money — a job packed inside one leaf never touches the spine, so a topology-aware scheduler recovers bandwidth that oversubscription took away.

Rail-optimized designs take this further: each of a node's eight NICs connects to its own "rail" of switches, so same-rank GPUs across nodes are one hop apart — cheaper than a full fat-tree, excellent for collectives, weaker for arbitrary traffic. The point isn't to memorize designs; it's to know the failure modes each one buys.

For an inference-first fleet, the tax mostly runs in reverse. The skill is declining to pay for fabric the workload won't touch, since most serving lives inside one node on NVLink. The exceptions are the ones growing: expert-parallel MoE serving puts an all-to-all on the network every layer, and disaggregated prefill/decode ships KV cache between pools over RDMA. The largest-model inference quietly re-acquires a training-shaped network bill.

Ask the supplierInfiniBand or Ethernet, which NIC generation, and what measured NCCL all-reduce bus bandwidth do you see at my job size — not line rate, measured?
Ask the supplierWhat's the oversubscription ratio, leaf to spine — and can your scheduler place my job within a single leaf or rail?
Ask the supplierFull fat-tree or rail-optimized? Show me the topology diagram, and tell me the blast radius when one spine switch fails.

This is what the fabric-factor tile is computing - delivered bandwidth as a fraction of best case, with effective price the sticker divided by it: two clusters with identical GPUs and identical sticker prices can differ 30%+ in delivered training throughput on wiring alone. Model here is illustrative — ring all-reduce, large-message regime, latency ignored, protocol efficiencies representative (IB ≈ 0.92, tuned RoCE ≈ 0.80 of line rate).
