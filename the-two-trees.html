The two trees

Instrument 03 priced the wiring; this one chooses its shape. The same GPUs can be wired as one fat tree — bandwidth preserved at every tier, any GPU to any GPU at full rate — or as eight skinny ones, each "rail" connecting the same-numbered GPU of every node. Rails cost a fraction as much and serve training traffic just as fast. The catch lives in the other two scenarios. Toggle and see.

Reading the trees like a buyer

Training traffic has a shape, and rails are wired to it. When NCCL runs an all-reduce, it builds rank-aligned rings: GPU-3 of node 12 exchanges with GPU-3 of nodes 11 and 13, and any data that must cross ranks moves inside each node over NVLink, where bandwidth is ~18× cheaper. So the network only ever sees same-rank pairs — eight independent streams, one per rail. A rail fabric gives each of those streams its own dedicated switch, one hop, full rate, and simply deletes the spine tier the fat-tree needed to guarantee the same thing for everyone.

The cost gap is countable. 512 GPUs with 64-port switches: a 1:1 fat-tree needs 16 leaves plus 16 spines and 1,024 optical links; rails need 8 switches and 512 links, with optics the dominant line item. Roughly 4× fewer switches and half the optics for identical delivered performance — on collective-shaped traffic.

What rails deleted is every path between different ranks on different nodes. Off-pattern traffic — mixture-of-experts all-to-all routing, embedding lookups, parameter-server flows, storage and data loading — must detour: hop the NVLink bridge inside the node to borrow a port on the right rail, then cross. The detour spends NVLink bandwidth the collectives also need, and turns first-class traffic into second-class. The fat-tree pays for generality; rails pay only for the pattern training uses, and everything off-pattern gets taxed.

Failure modes are shaped by the wiring too. A fat-tree leaf death is deep but narrow — its nodes drop, the rest run. A spine death is shallow and wide — capacity dips, nothing disconnects. A rail death is the strange one: no node goes down, but every node loses the same port simultaneously, and since an all-reduce finishes only when its slowest ring does, one failed box can stall every multi-node job in the cluster at once.

Ask the supplierFull fat-tree or rail-optimized — show me the topology diagram. And when one switch fails, what exactly keeps running: which nodes, which rails, which of my jobs?
Ask the supplierIf it's rails: is the scheduler rail-aware, what's the NCCL configuration, and what are your measured numbers for all-to-all — not just all-reduce — at my job size?
