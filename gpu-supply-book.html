How to read it

The model: average demand is fixed at 100 GPUs; on-demand capacity costs $6.00 per GPU-hour; reserved capacity costs $6.00 divided by the discount multiple, but is paid for every hour whether used or not. Everything under the demand curve is sellable; any committed capacity above the curve is paid for but sits idle.

commit a GPU ⇔ its expected utilization ≥ reserved ÷ on-demand price
at a 3× discount: breakeven = $2.00 / $6.00 = 33% of hours

The cost curve has a flat bottom. Sweep K from zero to max and watch blended cost fall, bottom out at the dashed optimal line, and rise. Note how flat the bottom is — being 20% wrong on book size barely costs anything. Real capacity books die from counterparty and duration risk, not mis-sizing.

Raise volatility. Hold the discount and raise peak-to-average. The optimal line drops and the amber on-demand share grows: spikier demand means committing less, regardless of how good the discount is. When peak-to-average far exceeds the discount multiple, even the optimal book barely beats pure on-demand.

Raise the discount. Hold volatility and raise the discount. Breakeven utilization is just one over the discount, so a deeper discount makes it rational to commit further into rarely-used layers. Every turn of discount negotiated changes how much of the demand curve the book can afford to own.

One honest limitation: this demand curve is known and static. The real job prices an uncertain curve growing several-fold a year, which pushes optimal behavior toward shorter durations and paid-for flexibility beyond what this picture shows.
