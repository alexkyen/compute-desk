Reading the spec sheet like a buyer

The comparability trap. Headline FLOPS include sparsity and shift numeric precision every generation — Hopper's marketed number is FP8, Blackwell's and Rubin's are FP4 — so cross-generation compute multiples partly measure the arithmetic getting narrower, not just the silicon getting faster. Memory capacity and bandwidth are the honest cross-generation rulers, which is convenient, because for inference they are usually the binding ones.

The depreciation clock. The cadence is now annual — Blackwell 2024, Blackwell Ultra 2025, Rubin 2026, Rubin Ultra promised 2027 — and each launch resets the resale and renewal value of everything above it on this page. Historically, prices of the incumbent fall 40–50% over 12–18 months from supply expansion, then further when the successor ships in volume. Every multi-year commitment is short this clock.

The constraint migrated. From B200 onward the practical scarcity isn't chips — it's watts and plumbing. Rack power densities that force liquid cooling mean the question "can you get GPUs?" quietly became "can your facility host them?", which is why datacenter readiness now belongs in supplier diligence alongside price.

† Rubin figures are pre-volume: bandwidth spec is 22 TB/s with early shipments reported nearer 20 as HBM4 suppliers chase NVIDIA's pin speeds, and rack power is not final. HBM4 supply gates the ramp; first allocation goes to hyperscalers and frontier labs.
