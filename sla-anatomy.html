SLA anatomy

A service-level agreement is an insurance contract, so read it like one: what counts as a claim, over what window it's measured, and what's excluded. The nines on the cover page are marketing; the value lives in three clauses. Stage a month's worth of failures below and see what the paper actually pays.

The negotiator's dictionary

The calculator above covers availability, windows, and credits. These are the other clauses that move money — grouped by what they're for.

availability & credits — beyond the calculator

cure period — the time the provider gets to fix a breach before credits or termination rights can be invoked; vague or uncapped cure language quietly neuters every remedy downstream of it.

maintenance-window notice — the negotiable substance behind the exclusions checkbox: how much advance notice "scheduled maintenance" requires, how many hours per month it may consume, and who unilaterally decides an incident qualifies.

right-to-audit — the ability to independently verify the provider's uptime and failure logs instead of trusting a self-reported number; the answer to "who measures, and how" that boilerplate SLAs never resolve.

delivery & capacity

firm delivery commitment vs. allocation priority — language that promises pricing and queue position but no enforceable delivery date with consequences is an option on capacity, not a contract for it. The single highest-leverage distinction in a 2026 GPU deal.

take-or-pay — commit to fixed volume at a fixed rate for the term and pay whether you use it or not. The standard neocloud instrument — and the reason exit rights are hard to get: your committed cash flow is the collateral behind the provider's debt.

burn-in / acceptance testing — pre-delivery stress testing (HBM, NVLink, fabric) with defined pass criteria before your billing clock starts. Ask what the criteria are and who signs off.

hot-spare pool — standby nodes reserved against your cluster specifically, so failures swap from dedicated spares instead of queueing behind the general fleet.

time-to-replace (TTR) — a contractual maximum on failed-node replacement. A number, not "best effort" or "commercially reasonable."

utilization floor — the level below which you're billed anyway, and what happens to pricing beneath it. A published spot market well below on-demand signals genuine spare capacity; spot glued to on-demand — or absent — signals a fully committed fleet.

data residency / sovereignty commitment — a contractual, not marketing, guarantee of where data and compute physically sit and which jurisdiction governs.

pricing mechanics

commitment-discount naming — the same lever wearing four badges: AWS Capacity Blocks / Savings Plans, GCP CUDs, Azure reservations under EA/MACC, Oracle Universal Credits. Knowing the local name is table stakes.

most-favored-nation (MFN) — a guarantee you never pay more than a comparable customer at comparable volume and term, ideally with an audit right. Expect friction; it's the direct counter to bespoke anchor pricing.

true-up / true-down (ramp schedule) — the right to adjust committed volume at defined checkpoints in a multi-year term without repricing penalties, rather than living with your year-one guess.

price-lock vs. index-linked pricing — whether the rate is fixed for the term or floats with inputs (energy above all). Ask which one you're signing; the answer is often buried.

exit & risk

termination for convenience vs. for cause — leave-anytime (rare here, for the take-or-pay financing reason above) versus leave-only-on-defined-breach (the default).

termination for chronic breach — an exit trigger keyed to a pattern (N breaches in M months) rather than one severe incident; the clause that makes a string of "minor" months actionable.

assignment / change-of-control — what happens to your contract if the provider is acquired, restructures, or fails — and whether you can assign if you are. In a consolidating market this outranks most of the SLA.

liability cap + exclusive remedy — the pairing that locks in "credits are all you get": a damages cap plus language making service credits your sole remedy. The legal mechanism behind everything the calculator shows.

the trap'Credits as sole remedy' plus a liability cap is the whole ballgame; the nines are decoration around it.

Reading the paper like a buyer

The definition clause is worth more than the nines. Most SLAs define "unavailable" at the platform or region level: the API is down, everything is down. But the dominant failure mode of a GPU fleet is chronic and node-level — a GPU falls off the bus, HBM throws errors, a NIC flaps — and under a platform-level definition, none of it registers on the availability metric. Meta's Llama 3 team reported 419 unexpected interruptions in 54 days of training on 16,384 H100s, roughly one hardware failure every three hours, overwhelmingly at the node level. Under boilerplate SLA language, that entire experience measures as 100% uptime. And it isn't a training-run anecdote: Modal, operating a 20,000 GPU multi-cloud pool, reports the same node-level texture - ECC errors, thermal violations, hardware slowdowns - and concludes "it's underappreciated how unreliable GPUs are." Toggle the definition above and watch the same month of pain flip between zero credits and six figures.

The measurement window is where the lawyers hid the value. A one-hour outage is a 99.86% month — a breach of a 99.9% monthly commitment — but a 99.989% year, no breach at all. Same incident, same headline nines, different clause, different payout. Annual measurement dilutes every incident by a factor of twelve; monthly measurement is the buyer's floor position.

Credits are shaped to pay on the rare event and never on the chronic one. Play with the sliders and you'll notice something honest: for a total platform outage, tiered credits (10–100% of monthly fees) can look adequate, even generous, against the direct GPU-hour loss. That's by design — total outages are rare, capped at one month's fees, claims-gated, and often excluded, so they're cheap to insure. The chronic node-level failures that actually dominate the cost of running GPUs pay nothing. And credits are service credits against future bills, not cash — worth exactly zero if the relationship is ending, which is the moment you'd most want them.

What actually protects a serious customer isn't the availability SLA at all — it's operational commitments: time-to-replace on a failed node, hot-spare pools held against your cluster, burn-in and acceptance testing before delivery, and termination rights on chronic breach. Those clauses cost the provider real money to honor, which is precisely why they signal fleet quality better than any number of nines on the cover page.

Ask the supplierDefine "unavailable" for me — does it include a single node down, or degraded interconnect performance, or only full loss of the API? And what was your fleet's node-level annualized failure rate last quarter?
Ask the supplierMeasured monthly or annually? Read me the exclusions list. And what's the claims process — automatic, or do I file with logs inside 30 days?
Ask the supplierForget credits: what's your committed time-to-replace on a failed node, do you hold hot spares against my cluster, and do I get termination rights on chronic breach?
